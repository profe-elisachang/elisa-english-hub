Despite these health benefits, convincing the public to embrace the idea of consuming "good bacteria" initially proved to be a formidable challenge. This marketing approach proved successful even outside Japan, especially in South Korea. The first South Korean Yakult Ladies began selling on the streets in 1971. (A) Their early delivery work was quite physically demanding. They often had to push heavy carts filled with ice to keep the products cool. A dramatic change occurred in 2014, when Korea Yakult introduced the CoCo, a motorized refrigerated cart. This new vehicle took the physical burden off the women. (E) Furthermore, with its increased storage capacity, their offerings could also be expanded. They now provide a wider array of products, including coffee and juice.

Today, being a Yakult Lady is more than just a sales job. It has grown into a form of community service. (D) Since 1972, the women in Japan have been conducting Courtesy Visit Activities. During these interactions, deliveries to seniors living alone double as a chance to check on their well-being and engage in friendly chats. This community-focused mission also took hold in South Korea. There, Yakult Ladies perform daily wellness checks for low-income seniors who live alone, ensuring their health and promptly reporting emergencies. In a fast-paced and often disconnected world, the Yakult Lady is a warm, familiar presence.
