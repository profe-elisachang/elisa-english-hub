üìñ Article: Tetrachromacy and Color Perception

Most people perceive the world through trichromatic vision, which means their eyes contain three types of cone cells that can detect red, green, and blue wavelengths of light. These cones combine signals to generate the full spectrum of colors in our environment. This system allows for millions of color combinations and serves us well in daily life.

For a rare few individuals, though, the world appears even more colorful due to a condition called tetrachromacy. People with this trait possess a fourth type of cone cell in the retina. This additional cone often results from a genetic variation linked to the X chromosome. It can increase color sensitivity and allow a person to distinguish subtle variations in color that trichromats cannot perceive. In practical terms, this means what looks like a single shade to most people might appear as several distinct hues to a tetrachromat. Although tetrachromacy is extremely rare, it occurs more frequently in women since they have two X chromosomes and therefore a higher chance of carrying this trait.

One of the most fascinating cases of a tetrachromat involves an artist called Concetta Antico. Her paintings burst with color, reflecting the way she sees the world. According to Antico, something as plain as a dull gray stone might shimmer with hints of orange, yellow, pink, green, and blue. She describes her visual experience as "a kaleidoscope of color." Her heightened sensitivity gives her the ability to identify the ripest fruit at a glance or notice changes in color that are invisible to most people.
