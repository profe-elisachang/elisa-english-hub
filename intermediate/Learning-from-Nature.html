üìñ Article: Nature's Building Secrets

Architects have long admired the beauty of bamboo. Recently, they've also started to see it as a model for load-bearing structures. Hollow bamboo stems are divided into sections by solid nodes that strengthen the plant, enabling it to withstand strong winds. Now, drawing on this principle, engineers are designing better skyscrapers.

Architects have also borrowed from the structure of trees. These plants have layers of fibers that spiral around a core, almost like a rope, to create a flexible trunk. Thanks to this, trees can sway rather than snap in the wind. The steeper the angle of the spirals, the more the tree can bend. Because of this, the lower parts of a tree, which rely on strength and stability, have steeply angled spirals. The higher parts, on the other hand, have spirals at shallower angles that sacrifice some strength for flexibility. Designers have used these ideas to build constructions that blend strength with flexibility.

Plants have other traits that are valuable for design. For example, the shells of nuts have a hard, outer layer and a soft, inner layer that work together to resist cracks. When something strikes the shell, the hard layer keeps the blow from penetrating too deeply. If it does manage to crack the hard layer, the soft layer absorbs the force of the impact and keeps the crack from spreading. Researchers are now creating new materials that copy this structure.
