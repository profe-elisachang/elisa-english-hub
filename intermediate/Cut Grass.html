The moment grass is cut, the air is instantly filled with a crisp (fresh, clean, and pleasant), distinctive aroma that is almost universally recognized. To many, it may seem pleasant‚Äîeven more appealing than scented candles or fancy perfumes. Yet what we are inhaling is actually a desperate call for help, a signal plants send out when they are under attack.

The smell of freshly cut grass mainly comes from a group of compounds known as green leaf volatiles (GLVs). When grasses are bitten by insects or cut by a lawnmower, they give off these substances that spread rapidly through the air. Studies have shown that GLVs help stimulate new cell growth for speedy repair and may serve as natural antibiotics to fight off bacterial infections. More surprisingly, GLVs are a key survival strategy for the plant. They can attract predator insects that feed on plant-eating pests. Meanwhile, nearby plants can pick up these airborne warnings and strengthen their own defenses, readying themselves for possible threats. While almost every plant releases GLVs when they are injured, the smell can differ depending on the species.

To humans, however, this emergency signal means something entirely different. Scientists believe the smell pleases us partly because it overlaps with the aromas of chopped vegetables or ripening fruit, triggering food-related instincts. Deeply etched in our olfactory memory, the scent also calls to mind mowed lawns, haymaking in the countryside, or weekend sports played on grassy fields. Unsurprisingly, it is often described as uplifting. Research even shows that this aroma can reduce stress. Still, not everyone enjoys it. For allergy sufferers, the aroma is less about comfort and more about sneezing and watery eyes.
